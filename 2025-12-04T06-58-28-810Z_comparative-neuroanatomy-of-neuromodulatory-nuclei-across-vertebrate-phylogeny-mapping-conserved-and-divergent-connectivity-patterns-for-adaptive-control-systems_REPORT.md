## Executive Summary

# Executive Summary: Comparative neuroanatomy of neuromodulatory nuclei across vertebrate phylogeny: mapping conserved and divergent connectivity patterns for adaptive control systems

Comparative neuroanatomy reveals deep evolutionary conservation of key neuromodulatory nuclei and circuits across vertebrate phylogeny, alongside lineage-specific adaptations. The habenular complex, a phylogenetically ancient diencephalic structure, and its projection to the interpeduncular nucleus via the fasciculus retroflexus represent one of the most conserved connectivity patterns from fish to mammals. Similarly, the locus coeruleus (LC), a vertebrate-specific noradrenergic nucleus, maintains a conserved core architecture and widespread, collateralized projections, functioning as a crucial regulatory hub. These structures, along with the four primary ascending neuromodulatory systems (acetylcholine, norepinephrine, dopamine, serotonin), form a conserved framework for adaptive behavioral control.

Important conserved relationships include the habenula's role as an integrative hub linking forebrain cognitive/emotional networks (like the basal ganglia and amygdala) to brainstem neuromodulatory centers, guiding value-based decisions. The periaqueductal gray (PAG) also serves as a conserved integration node for higher-order inputs. While core circuits are preserved, significant evolutionary divergence exists. The lateral habenula shows expanded connectivity with limbic and prefrontal regions in tetrapods, thalamocortical reciprocity increases substantially in amniotes, and structures like the vestibular efferent nucleus exhibit clear phylogenetic reorganization across vertebrate classes.

Current research establishes robust homologies for core structures but highlights gaps in understanding the functional consequences of conserved anatomy versus divergent connectivity, especially in non-mammalian models. A key limitation is the variable granularity of connectivity maps across species. Next steps should involve functional comparative studies to link these anatomical patterns to adaptive behaviors and investigate how conserved neuromodulatory hubs integrate with lineage-specific forebrain elaborations, such as the mammalian neocortex.

  Evidence: The LC is identified as a 'vertebrate-specific nucleus' and a 'crucial regulatory hub' across species. It serves as the primary source of norepinephrine, with extensive projections to the forebrain, brainstem, cerebellum, and spinal cord. This projection pattern is conserved from fish (projecting to telencephalon, optic tectum, hippocampus) to amniotes, with additional regions like the substantia nigra appearing in amphibians and reptiles.

- Major changes in thalamocortical connectivity mark a key evolutionary transition, with substantial reciprocal connections appearing in amniotes.
  Evidence: In fishes, the thalamus is small with little interaction with the telencephalon, receiving retinal input and connecting with tectum and hypothalamus. A major evolutionary change was the increase in reciprocal connections between thalamus and telencephalon, which become substantial in amniotes (reptiles, birds, mammals).

- The periaqueductal gray (PAG) serves as a conserved integration hub receiving convergent inputs from higher-order brain areas across vertebrates.
  Evidence: The PAG receives a confluence of inputs from higher-order brain areas, with the ventromedial hypothalamus (VMH) input being interconnected with over 30 brain areas—a pattern conserved across all major vertebrate groups. The PAG integrates inputs from amygdala, hypothalamus, and prefrontal cortex to regulate context-appropriate instinctive behaviors.

- Basal ganglia organization shows both conserved and divergent features across vertebrate evolution.
  Evidence: Historical models suggested sequential addition of components: globus pallidus in fish, striatum in amphibians, and rudimentary cerebral cortex in reptiles. While basal ganglia expanded in reptiles and birds, mammals elaborated the cerebral cortex into neocortex. The ventral basal ganglia loops involved in motivation and reward receive conserved inputs from prefrontal cortex and pallial amygdala.

- Neuromodulatory systems (noradrenergic, cholinergic, serotonergic, dopaminergic) form conserved ascending systems regulating brain-wide states.
  Evidence: The LC receives inputs from multiple brainstem nuclei and projects widely to cortex, hippocampus, thalamus, amygdala, and cerebellum. Cholinergic nuclei (PPT, LDT) receive conserved afferents from reticular formation, periaqueductal gray, and hypothalamic regions. These systems are essential for regulating transitions between distinct behavioral and brain states.

- The nitrergic neuronal system shows a conserved organizational pattern from primitive bony fishes to amniotes.
  Evidence: Studies in cladistian fishes (basal actinopterygians) reveal a nitrergic system organization that is conserved through to amniotes and mammals when analyzed using neuromeric frameworks, indicating deep phylogenetic conservation of this neuromodulatory system architecture.

### Artifact 3: Comparative neuroanatomy of neuromodulatory nuclei across vertebrate phylogeny: mapping conserved and divergent connectivity patterns for adaptive control systems - Iteration 3

- The habenular complex is a phylogenetically ancient and highly conserved diencephalic structure present in all vertebrates, consistently organized into medial (MHb) and lateral (LHb) divisions.
  Evidence: Multiple sources describe the habenula as a "phylogenetically old brain structure present in all vertebrates" with conserved medial and lateral divisions across species from lampreys to mammals. The structure is recognized as part of the epithalamus and shows remarkable conservation in basic organization.

- The core habenulo-interpeduncular circuit (MHb→IPN via fasciculus retroflexus) represents one of the most conserved connectivity patterns in vertebrate neuroanatomy.
  Evidence: Evidence from lampreys, zebrafish, amphibians, and mammals shows consistent MHb projections to the interpeduncular nucleus (IPN). The fasciculus retroflexus is described as the "core" pathway maintained from fish to mammals, with the IPN existing even in hagfishes and lampreys based on habenular input.

- Lateral habenula connectivity shows greater evolutionary divergence and increased complexity in tetrapods, particularly in its integration with basal ganglia and limbic systems.
  Evidence: While the LHb maintains connections with brainstem monoaminergic nuclei (dopaminergic SN/VTA, serotonergic raphe) across vertebrates, tetrapods show expanded connectivity with basal ganglia structures (nucleus accumbens, ventral pallidum), limbic forebrain, and prefrontal regions not as prominent in fish.

- Habenular asymmetry is pronounced in many fish species but reduced in most amniotes, with functional implications for lateralized behaviors.
  Evidence: Sources describe "conspicuous" habenular asymmetry in agnathans, cartilaginous and bony fishes, often involving both medial and lateral domains. In amniotes (birds, mammals), asymmetries are "much less pronounced" except in some exceptions like lizards. Zebrafish show left-right segregation of habenular outputs along the IPN dorsoventral axis.

- The habenula serves as a conserved integrative hub linking cognitive/emotional networks with brainstem neuromodulatory systems for adaptive behavioral control.
  Evidence: The habenula is described as "integrating cognitive, emotional and sensory networks to regulate mood, motivation and value-based decision-making" and as "a link between basal ganglia and limbic system" that guides "effective response strategies appropriate to environmental conditions" across vertebrates.

- Subnuclear organization of habenular complexes shows both conserved features and lineage-specific specializations, with increased complexity in mammals.
  Evidence: While basic medial/lateral division is conserved, mammalian habenulae show more elaborate subnuclear organization (15 subnuclei described in rats) with distinct cytochemical, hodological, and functional properties. Transcriptional profiling reveals finer cell type distinctions in mice compared to anatomical subnuclei.
