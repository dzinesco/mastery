## Executive Summary

# Executive Summary: "Evolutionary transcriptomics of neuromodulatory cell types: linking gene regulatory networks to conserved and species-specific connectivity patterns in vertebrate brains"

This research synthesizes how gene regulatory networks (GRNs) determine the identity of neuromodulatory cell types, revealing a core set of conserved neuronal types and circuits across vertebrates. A foundational architecture, including major systems like dopamine and serotonin, is preserved from fish to mammals. Key structures such as the habenular nuclei and the locus coeruleus represent highly conserved regulatory hubs, indicating that basic neural blueprints for modulating brain-wide states are evolutionarily ancient.

The conservation is orchestrated by GRNs, where transcription factors and cis-regulatory elements control cell fate and connectivity patterns. These networks regulate axon guidance and cell adhesion molecules, establishing both shared features and species-specific variations. For instance, while core circuit motifs are maintained, fine-scale connectivity and some cell types diverge, as seen in the simplified zebrafish dopaminergic system compared to mammals. Transcription factors also maintain neuronal identity into adulthood, with disruptions leading to behavioral deficits.

Significant gaps remain in mapping the complete cis-regulatory grammar for neuromodulatory subtypes and understanding how species-specific connectivity emerges from conserved GRNs. Current models rely heavily on zebrafish and mouse; expanding comparative transcriptomics to more diverse vertebrates is crucial. Next steps involve systematic perturbation of regulatory elements to directly link genetic programs to conserved versus divergent wiring, ultimately explaining how shared developmental rules generate both universal circuits and adaptive specializations.

### Artifact 2: "Evolutionary transcriptomics of neuromodulatory cell types: linking gene regulatory networks to conserved and species-specific connectivity patterns in vertebrate brains" - Iteration 2

- Gene regulatory networks (GRNs) composed of transcription factors and cis-regulatory elements determine the identity and connectivity of neuromodulatory cell types.
  Evidence: GRNs are defined as molecular regulators governing gene expression levels, determining cell function and playing central roles in morphogenesis and evolutionary developmental biology. Studies in zebrafish and C. elegans show distinct transcription factors determine neuronal identity. The ETS transcription factor Etv4 regulates cell body position, axonal arborization, and dendritic targeting in motor neurons. In serotonergic neurons, the terminal selector transcription factor Pet-1 orchestrates maturation, axon guidance, and terminal field recognition, working with co-activators and repressors to establish neuron-specific features and generate heterogeneity within the cell type.

- Conserved neuromodulatory circuits exist across vertebrate phylogeny, particularly in the habenular nuclei and locus coeruleus.
  Evidence: The habenular nuclei and their connectivity to the interpeduncular nucleus represent a highly conserved circuit across vertebrate phylogeny, maintained from fish to mammals. The medial habenula to IPN projection is described as the 'core' of the fasciculus retroflexus and is conserved in all vertebrate species examined. The locus coeruleus is a vertebrate-specific noradrenergic nucleus with conserved core architecture and widespread, highly collateralized projections across vertebrate phylogeny, described as a 'crucial regulatory hub across various vertebrate species' with similar though not identical appearance across species.

- Transcription factors regulate both conserved and species-specific aspects of neuronal connectivity through control of axon guidance molecules and cell adhesion proteins.
  Evidence: Transcriptomic analysis of dorsal raphe serotonin neuron subtypes shows differential expression of genes involved in specifying neuronal connectivity, including cell adhesion and axon guidance molecules. The ETS transcription factor Etv4 regulates neuronal morphology through regulation of cadherins and semaphorins. Studies reveal that multiple transcription factors control axon guidance in a subset-specific manner, echoing the modular control of neuronal identity where transcription factors act in subset-specific ways to regulate connectivity patterns.

- Four primary neuromodulatory systems (acetylcholine, norepinephrine, dopamine, serotonin) form conserved ascending systems that regulate brain-wide states across vertebrates.
  Evidence: Multiple sources identify these four molecules as the primary neuromodulatory systems originating in brainstem/midbrain/basal forebrain and projecting broadly to modulate synaptic transmission patterns and brain-wide activity. They are described as essential for regulating transitions between distinct behavioral and brain states, with conserved functions across vertebrate species. These systems employ similar mechanisms, such as the thalamic reticular nucleus and neuromodulators like acetylcholine being critical for regulating thalamocortical dynamics and brain state transitions.

- Transcription factors maintain neuronal identity in adulthood and their disruption has behavioral consequences.
  Evidence: Recent studies in mammalian models demonstrate roles for developmentally important transcription factors in maintaining distinct neuronal subtypes in adulthood. Conditional knockdown of Nurr1 (key for midbrain dopaminergic neurons) results in loss of dopaminergic neurons and impaired motor behavior. Conditional knockout of Pet-1 in serotonergic neurons elevates anxiety behaviors in mice and is required for 5-HT synthesis in the adult brain, highlighting the clinical implications of understanding maintenance of neuronal identity through transcriptional regulation.
